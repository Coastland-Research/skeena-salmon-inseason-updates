Skeena Salmon In-Season Update

August 17, 2024

Summary

  • Pre-season forecast

    • The pre-season forecast for the Total Return of Skeena sockeye in 2024 ranges from 0.58 to 4.0 million (90% confidence range) with a point estimate (p50) of 1.54 million. The forecast for 2024 includes a low return of age-4 sockeye, and an average return of age-5 sockeye.

  • Tyee Test Fishery

    • Tides influence the migration of salmon into the Skeena River.

    • To date ~1.69M sockeye are estimated to have passed by Tyee, with about 93% of the run through to date on average (87% if 1-week late, 97% if one week early). Daily sockeye counts have dropped and are now between 2-5,000 (Figures 1-5). Including commercial catch and First Nations marine catches, the TRTC estimate is around 1.9M with about 200,000 marine commercial catch between Area C, Area A and First Nations Demo/EO fisheries.

    • The excel model increased in early July, stabilized briefly, decreased significantly, and stabilized again, declined slowly, and since has stabilized again although slowly declining. The 3 lines are converging as is normal this time of year and this also supports average timing of the run.

    • The current run size prediction is 2.0M on average run-timing (2.1M if one week late and 1.9M if 1 week early) (Figure 6). These should not change much anymore through the remainder of the run.

    • The Chinook index remains very low and available evidence suggests a very low run and is tracking near the 10th percentile (Figure 7).

    • The chum salmon index is currently lower than average between the 25th and 50th percentile (Figure 8).

    • Pink salmon are looking strong this year relative to even-year abundance with the index tracking around the 90th percentile (Figure 9).

    • Coho salmon are passing through Tyee and tracking just under the long term average (Figure 10).

    • The Steelhead index suggests a strong run is continuing, around the 90th percentile. The latest Province of BC update estimates around 29,000 Steelhead through Tyee to date (as of August 8th) (Figure 11).

  • Babine Fence

    • The Babine River fence started operations on July 9th. The total large sockeye run to date is ~ 1.1M with recent large return days bumping the run over the recent and historical averages, and exceeding 2023 (Figures 12-14).

    • The total run of jack sockeye to date is now over 62k, well above the longer term and recent averages (Figures 15-16).

  • Babine versus Tyee comparison

    • Figure 17 shows the expected difference between the escapement past Tyee and the number of sockeye showing up at the Babine fence. This includes the in-river catch and an adjustment assuming that 10% of the sockeye passing Tyee are non-Babine.

    • Although the comparison shows some delay in migration at the start of the run, there is nothing concerning at this point with the Babine fence tracking the expected run at 3 weeks migration timing quite closely. If large daily coutns continue, then we could see more large sockeye showing up at Babine than expected.

    • Figures (18-21) show the returns of species other than sockeye to the Babine fence versus historical averages.

  • Kitwanga Fence

    • The Kitwanga fence started operations July 10.
    • Details on the Kitwanga fence counts can be found at the Gitanyow Fisheries Authority website.

  • Commercial Fisheries

    • Commercial fishing has started, with Area C gillnet openings on July 13, 20-21, 25-26, 29-30, August 2-3 and 8-9. The Area A seine fishery was open July 25-28 and August 2-5. Coastal Demonstration Fisheries opened on July 16, 21, 24, 27-28, July 31-August 2, August 4-7 and 11. In-river demonstration fisheries started July 30 for the Gitksan and August 15 for LBN.

    • Preliminary sockeye catch in the Area C gillnet openings is ~ 132,000. Preliminary sockeye catch in the Area A seine fisheries is ~ 46,000. Preliminary demonstration fishery catches are over 25,000 sockeye and split between the coast and in-river. Late timed commercial openings had low effort and low catch rates.

    • More details on catch can be found in the catch section below and in Appendix B.

In-Season TRTC Estimates

This section shows the estimated final TRTC as predicted by the simple run-timing proportion Excel based spreadsheet tool. Accuracy of this model increases as the season progresses since run-timing uncertainty is large at the start of the year, and small at the end of the year. Catchability (how many sockeye each sockeye caught in the Tyee test fishery represents), the other component of the uncertainty in this model, is unknown until after the final TRTC accounting has been completed in the post-season. Tyee estimates typically have a range of +/- 20% from the true TRTC, with some years being greater than 20% error, and many years being less than 20% error. The 2024 catchability is based on an average of the last 3 years.

Babine Fence

The Babine fence was installed and fish tight on July 9. Data from the Babine fence is provided nightly via email from the LBN Fisheries program.

There are concerns at the Babine River about high temperatures and low water levels holding up passage of sockeye and other salmon through the fence. In 2024, LBN has implemented the Babine River Collaborative Management Plan and has choosen to voluntarily move their harvest from the Babine fence to Babine Lake.

First Nations Demonstration and Economic Opportunity

First Nations CSAF demonstration fisheries opened on July 16 in 2024. The catch associated with each demonstration fishery is determined using the weekly TAC. In 2024, the CTAC approach is being implemented, which means that the catch allocation of the demonstration fishery is no longer directly linked to what the commercial marine fishery catches each week.
